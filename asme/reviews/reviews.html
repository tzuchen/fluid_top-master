This paper seeks numerical evidence for cutoffs in chaotic
micro-fluidic mixing, where cutoffs are defined in terms of
a sharp convergence of a discrete Markov chain to a
steady-state solution, especially if this convergence
becomes sharper with increasing order of the Markov chain.
I've been asked to review it as a reviewer who has worked
for some time in the dynamic systems and control theory
areas, but is not an expert in microfluidics. With this in
mind, my review will focus mostly on the structure and
clarity of the paper, rather than its technical content. 

It is very difficult for me to discern the strengths and
contributions of this paper, because its writing is poor
and its structure unfortunately poorer. The authors present
a formal definition of cutoffs, then decide not to use it
in their work because, as they state, it is difficult to
define a cutoff. If it is difficult to define a cutoff, why
did the authors define it? The authors also make very
ambiguous claims such as "people believe cutoffs happen
more frequently than we thought". What does this statement
mean, exactly, and are the authors claiming that cutoffs
exist, or that they don't? The authors also present many of
their equations and symbols without definition, making the
paper very difficult to follow and understand. It is also
not clear why the paper examines cutoffs for different maps
(Baker's map, standard map, etc.), and what the
significance of those maps is. The paper's background
section presents some very basic definitions from
probability space theory, but the connection between these
definitions and fluid flow theory is neither clear from the
context nor explained by the authors. For all of the above
reasons, I cannot strongly recommend this paper for
publication as a reviewer. This is unfortunate, because the
paper may (or may not) contain important contributions to
the literature, but the paper's very poor structure and
presentation make it difficult for me to discern the
paper's contributions as a reviewer. Even if the paper is
very accessible to researchers in the microfluidic chaos
community, it is not very accessible to researchers outside
that community, and is hence unlikely to reach a very wide
audience.

This paper is a bit peripheral to the invited session but
it is still a solid paper. It relates the chaotic
microﬂuidic mixing with a cutoff phenomenon observed
in ﬁnite Markov chains. 

The mixing strategy studied is passive, in the sense that
it is influenced by fixed system design and hence is not
tunable (certainly not easily) achieve different design
objectives. The authors should acknowledge the existence of
feedback strategies for mixing in boundary-controlled
Navier-Stokes systems in various geometries, such as in 

O. M. Aamo, M. Krstic, and T. R. Bewley, ``Control of
mixing by boundary feedback in 2D channel flow,''  
Automatica, vol. 39, pp. 1597-1606, 2003.

A. Balogh, O. M. Aamo and M. Krstic, ``Optimal mixing
enhancement in 3D pipe flow,''	IEEE Transactions on
Control Systems Technology, vol. 13, pp. 27-41, 2005.

O.-M. Aamo and M. Krstic, ``Feedback control of particle
dispersion in bluff body wakes,''  International Journal of
Control, vol 77, pp. 1001-1018, 2004.

C.C. Yuan, M. Krstic, and T. Bewley, ``Active control of
jet mixing,'' IEE Proceedings: Control Theory and
Applications, vol. 151, pp. 763-772, 2004.

O. M. Aamo and M. Krstic, Flow Control by Feedback,
Springer, 2002.
